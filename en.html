Weak-to-Strong Generalization via Direct On-Policy Distillation

Transfer the improvement direction learned by a small RL model to stronger students,
instead of asking the student to imitate a weaker teacher.

Direct-OPD uses the policy shift discovered by RL as dense supervision on the student's own on-policy states.

RLVR has already shown that reinforcement learning can improve reasoning models, but scaling it to every larger model means repeating exploration under sparse outcome rewards. Direct-OPD reuses exploration that a small model has already completed: first let the small model learn a useful direction through RL, then apply that direction to the stronger model's own on-policy states.

Method: transfer the policy shift, not the teacher itself

The issue is not distillation, but what gets distilled

Standard OPD asks the student to match the teacher distribution on states visited by the student. When the teacher is stronger, this target is natural. In weak-to-strong transfer, however, the teacher has been improved by RL but is still a small model. Its final distribution carries two signals at once:

  • useful reasoning preferences learned through RL;
  • the capacity limit of the small model itself.

On R1-Distill-7B, the student starts above JustRL-1.5B. After standard OPD, performance does not keep improving; it falls back toward the teacher. The RL gain in JustRL-1.5B is useful, but directly imitating the post-RL teacher distribution transfers that gain together with the small model's ceiling.

Direct-OPD keeps only the teacher's change before and after RL

Direct-OPD uses a pair of teacher checkpoints: the pre-RL reference model $\pi_{T_{\mathrm{ref}}}$ and the post-RL teacher $\pi_T$. The student does not need to match $\pi_T$; the training signal comes from the distributional difference induced by RL.

$$\Delta_T(y \mid x)=\log \pi_T(y \mid x)-\log \pi_{T_{\mathrm{ref}}}(y \mid x)$$

If a token becomes more likely after RL, it aligns with the direction learned by the small model; if it becomes less likely, RL has suppressed that direction. This log-ratio can be used directly as a dense token-level implicit reward.

The student still follows its own path

During training, the student samples its own rollouts. For each visited prefix, Direct-OPD looks only at the top-k tokens the current student is most likely to consider, then scores those candidate tokens with the teacher-reference log-ratio.

The signal always lives on states actually visited by the student. The teacher does not need to generate full trajectories, and supervision becomes token-level instead of relying only on sparse final-answer correctness.

Experiment 1: directions learned by small-model RL improve stronger students

Direct-OPD improves multiple student models. The teacher does not need to be stronger than the student; what gets reused is the direction formed by RL.

Student AIME 2024 AIME 2025
Qwen3-1.7B 48.3 -> 62.4 36.8 -> 46.3
Qwen3-4B 72.5 -> 77.6 65.6 -> 68.8
R1-Distill-7B 56.7 -> 63.1 40.5 -> 48.8

Experiment 2: find the direction on a small model, then transfer it to a larger model

Running RL directly on a larger model asks that model to rediscover credit assignment through many rollouts. Direct-OPD moves this exploration to a small model, then transfers the resulting policy shift to the larger model.

Takeaway. Compared with direct large-model RL, running RL on a small model first and then transferring to the large model has two advantages: small-model RL is more stable, easier, and cheaper; the following Direct-OPD stage only needs short training to move that advantage to the larger model.

Experiment 3: directions learned by different RL processes can be composed sequentially

Direct-OPD transfers directions rather than the full post-RL teacher distribution. Policy shifts learned by different teacher pairs can therefore be applied sequentially to the same student.

Takeaway. Different RL training processes learn different capabilities. Direct-OPD can compose those capabilities as policy shifts inside the same student model.
